(01:22:04 PM) Michael Dillon: East coast friend: "We looked pretty hard at Play, but mostly Akka (Typesafe's magical scaling framework) and separately at Scala at my previous gig. We considered Java (not Scala) Play for the same reason anybody looks at, say, Rails and says "Hm, this seems like someone should really make one good way to do this stuff and avoid reinventing the wheel for every web application."

Scala is popular with all the Java folks who want the JVM libraries but wish they were writing Python or Go, but I don't see it as much of a revolution.

I've met a few of the execs at Typesafe - they seem to have equal interest in developing a good tech stack as they do selling consulting and training on that stack, and their Akka implementations often missed a lot of basic things you'd think would be important in a framework most people end up using for some kind of messaging/self-scaling async work system. Over time they've filled in the gaps, but they went years without any best-practice way to spin up a "guaranteed" message queue. Lots of ways for other semi-resilient types of work, but that was a gap I found kind of telling in their stack.

So, you'll see Scala picked up a lot and definitely be able to find work in it if that's your desire, it features some interesting language-level async primitives (Futures or Promises, I forget which ones they use), but I'm not convinced it's especially revolutionary, and I'm not entirely sure I trust the foundation that's pushing it since they have a vested interest in it being just short of flexible enough for anybody to really scale up into."
